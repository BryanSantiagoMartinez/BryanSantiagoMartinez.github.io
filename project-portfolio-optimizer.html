01 — Context

Why this period, and why these assets

The period 2020–2024 was not chosen arbitrarily. It captures four structurally different market regimes in five years — a dataset that forces any optimization model to prove itself beyond a single bull run.

The COVID crash of March 2020 was the fastest 34% drawdown in market history — 33 trading days. It broke correlations that had held for years. The 2020–2021 recovery was driven by near-zero interest rates and fiscal stimulus, producing one of the sharpest rebounds ever recorded. The 2022 bear market was structurally unusual: bonds and equities fell simultaneously — the worst year for the classic 60/40 portfolio in decades. The 2023–2024 rally was driven by a handful of AI-adjacent names, creating extreme concentration in broad indices.

A model that only works in rising markets is not a model. The value of any optimization framework is revealed precisely when regimes change.

The five assets represent fundamentally different economic exposures: technology growth (AAPL), financial cyclicality (JPM), defensive healthcare (JNJ), a commodity safe haven (GLD), and the broad market benchmark (SPY). The goal was not to cherry-pick assets that look good in hindsight — it was to test whether diversification works even when some assets disappoint.

03 — Correlation Structure

The real diversification story was in the correlations

When the full correlation matrix was computed, three findings stood out immediately — and each one directly shaped how the optimizer allocated capital.

PairCorrelationImplication
JPM ↔ GLD−0.01Near-perfect independence. Gold and banks respond to entirely different forces.
GLD ↔ SPY+0.16Gold barely co-moves with equities — classic safe-haven behavior confirmed.
AAPL ↔ SPY+0.79Tech moves with the market. AAPL + SPY adds concentration, not diversification.
JPM ↔ SPY+0.71Banks are cyclical — same regime exposure as the broad market.
AAPL ↔ JPM+0.68Both correlated with SPY — they amplify each other rather than offset.

Gold's near-zero correlation with JPM (−0.01) was the most consequential single finding. It meant the optimizer would systematically include GLD at a weight that return-only thinking would never suggest.

The second implication: AAPL and JPM are not true diversifiers of each other. Both correlate above 0.68 with SPY — holding both is a concentrated bet on the equity risk premium. This becomes directly visible in the optimal weights: the optimizer never simultaneously assigns high weight to both.

04 — Monte Carlo Simulation

Mapping the space of all possible portfolios

10,000 portfolios were generated using Dirichlet-distributed random weights — ensuring all combinations are positive and sum to exactly 100%. For each portfolio, annualized return, volatility and Sharpe Ratio were computed from the full covariance matrix.

01_portfolio.ipynb — Monte Carlo 
np.random.seed(42)
NUM_PORTFOLIOS = 10_000
results        = np.zeros((NUM_PORTFOLIOS, 3))

for i in range(NUM_PORTFOLIOS):
    # Dirichlet: positive weights that sum to 1
    w       = np.random.dirichlet(np.ones(num_assets))
    ret     = np.dot(w, mean_returns)
    vol     = np.sqrt(w.T @ cov_matrix @ w)
    results[i] = [ret, vol, ret / vol]

# Monte Carlo best Sharpe: 0.968 — approximation only
# True optimum requires constrained optimization (see §06)

The simulation found a best Sharpe of 0.968 — already higher than any individual asset. But Monte Carlo is an approximation: the best of 10,000 random samples, not the true mathematical optimum. The gap between 0.968 and the scipy result (0.979) is small but meaningful — it is the difference between a good guess and a provably optimal solution.

Note

Documenting this gap is intentional. Institutions use Monte Carlo for visualization, not for final allocation decisions. The cloud of portfolios builds intuition for where the optimum lies — constrained optimization finds it exactly.

05 — Efficient Frontier

What the cloud of portfolios reveals

When all 10,000 portfolios are plotted in risk-return space and colored by Sharpe Ratio, the structure of the feasible set becomes visible. Three observations stood out.

10,000 Monte Carlo portfolios · color = Sharpe ratio (red → amber → green)

First observation: both optimal portfolios sit on the upper-left boundary of the cloud — no greener point exists above or to their left. Second observation: JNJ is completely isolated below and to the right of the main cluster. Its low return combined with mid-range volatility places it in a dominated region — any optimizer with better alternatives will exclude it. Third observation: GLD sits at the far left of the cloud, well below all equity assets on the volatility axis. Individual assets always lie inside or below the frontier — the combination always dominates.

06 — scipy Optimization

From approximation to exact solution

SLSQP (Sequential Least Squares Programming) was applied with two constraints: no short selling (all weights ≥ 0%) and full investment (weights sum to exactly 100%). Two objective functions were solved independently — maximize Sharpe Ratio, and minimize portfolio volatility.

01_portfolio.ipynb — scipy SLSQP 
from scipy.optimize import minimize

def neg_sharpe(w):
    ret = np.dot(w, mean_returns)
    vol = np.sqrt(w.T @ cov_matrix @ w)
    return -(ret / vol)  # minimize negative = maximize sharpe

constraints = {'type': 'eq', 'fun': lambda w: np.sum(w) - 1}
bounds      = tuple((0, 1) for _ in range(num_assets))
w0          = np.ones(num_assets) / num_assets

result = minimize(neg_sharpe, w0, method='SLSQP',
                   bounds=bounds, constraints=constraints)

JPM at ~60% in both portfolios is striking given its high individual volatility (32.5%). The explanation lies in its return over this specific window — JPM recovered sharply from COVID and benefited from the 2022 rate hike cycle in ways GLD and JNJ did not. This is a direct reminder that optimization is always conditional on the lookback period.

The transition from Max Sharpe to Min Volatility tells a precise story: AAPL's 32.7% is replaced almost entirely by JNJ's 28.3%. When the objective shifts from maximizing risk-adjusted return to minimizing risk, high-volatility growth assets are replaced by defensive ones — regardless of their individual Sharpe. The optimizer is not ranking assets. It is solving a system of equations.

Note

SPY receives 0% in the Max Sharpe portfolio despite being the market benchmark. AAPL and JPM already have correlations of 0.79 and 0.71 with SPY — adding it would be redundant exposure without incremental return. The optimizer eliminates it as efficiently as it eliminated JNJ.

JNJ and SPY are eliminated entirely. Corner solutions reveal which assets are genuinely redundant given what the remaining three already provide.

The most counterintuitive result: GLD, with the second-lowest individual return, appears in both optimal portfolios. Return-only thinking would have excluded it. Correlation-aware optimization includes it precisely because every other asset moves together.

08 — Limitations & Next Steps

What this model does not do

Documenting limitations is not a weakness — it is what separates rigorous analysis from a demonstration.

  • Lookback biasWeights are optimized on historical data. A rolling window reoptimization — recalculating every quarter using only past data — would test whether the allocation generalizes out-of-sample.
  • Static weightsThe portfolio holds fixed allocations for 5 years. Correlations shift across regimes — particularly between the COVID crisis and the 2022 rate environment. A dynamic model rebalances as the covariance matrix evolves.
  • No costsTransaction costs and rebalancing fees are not modeled. These erode actual returns, especially for strategies with high turnover between periods.
  • NormalityLog returns are approximately but not perfectly normal. Fat tails exist — March 2020 was a 6-sigma event under a Gaussian assumption. Module 2 addresses this via CVaR, which does not assume normality.
